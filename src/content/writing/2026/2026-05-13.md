---
title: Sinking Costs
---
You've probably heard of the Sunken Cost Fallacy, which is a cognitive bias that causes people to continue putting time/effort/another type of investment into something that may not have a fruiful enough payoff simply because they have already invested significantly into this endeavor.

Yet, at what point should one decide when to stop investing into something? If this is something deterministic with a quantifiable outcome (e.g. an investment into a public company) then you can set a stop loss and exit the trade. But what if this isn't the case, and it is something more abstract, like a relationship or piece of writing? How can you know when to stop (or conversely when to keep going)? What factors do you use to measure cost and a  return on your investment? How do you weigh each factor? How do you know if what you are doing requires leverage, e.g. borrowing something (usually time) to keep the endeavor alive? There are probably more questions one can ask along this line, but they all have the same flavor.

Building a mental model of this can be helpful for more abstract things, and is something I've recently started thinking about.
